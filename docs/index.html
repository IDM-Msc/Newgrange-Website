Discover Newgrange

Newgrange is a Stone Age monument in the Boyne Valley and one of Ireland’s most important prehistoric sites. Built around 5,200 years ago, it predates both Stonehenge and the Great Pyramids of Giza.

Built by Stone Age farmers, the great circular mound measures approximately 85 metres in diameter and 13 metres high. A stone-built passage, 19 metres in length, leads to an inner chamber with three recesses.

K1

The mound is encircled by 97 large kerbstones, some of which are decorated with prehistoric carvings known as megalithic art, the most striking being the entrance stone, known as Kerbstone 1.

K52

The carved kerbstones around Newgrange show the skill and symbolism of Neolithic art. Spirals, curves, and geometric patterns appear across the monument, suggesting that decoration was an important part of its design as well as its meaning.

K67

A ring of 97 kerbstones surrounds the base of the mound, acting as both a retaining wall and a symbolic boundary. Some of these stones are decorated with intricate carvings, showing that the outer edge of Newgrange was designed to be both structural and ceremonial.

While archaeologists originally classified Newgrange as a passage tomb, it is now widely understood to be far more than a place of burial. It functioned as an ancient temple, with deep astrological, spiritual, religious, and ceremonial significance.

HISTORY OF NEWGRANGE

3500-3000 BC

(Middle Neolithic) The great passage tombs of Knowth, Newgrange and Dowth were built at Brú na Bóinne.

2500-800 BC

(Bronze Age) The Great Stone Circle at Newgrange may have been built early in this period. It is unlikely that the Late Neolithic ceremonial structures were entirely abandoned. However, from around 2000 BC to 100 BC there is little evidence for settlement, burial or ritual at the passage tombs.

800 BC-AD 400

(Iron Age) Burials began again at Knowth around 100 BC. Devotional objects have been found both there and at Newgrange, indicating that both had become important ceremonial sites again.

400-800

The final phase of burials at Knowth began about 600, coinciding with the early years of Christianity in Ireland and the emergence of powerful regional rulers.

1699

Local workmen rediscovered the chamber of the Great Mound at Newgrange.

1882

Knowth, Newgrange and Dowth were listed for protection under the Ancient Monuments Protection Act.

1900s

Archaeological investigation of the passage tombs in Brú na Bóinne began, continuing until the present.

1961

By 1961 Newgrange was in a dilapidated condition and, despite the rapidly increasing number of visitors, there was no right of public access. It was becoming clear to many that this, one of the greatest monuments in Western Europe, could not be left in its then condition, but urgently required restoration and conservation, at least in the entrance area.

1963

At Newgrange, O’Kelly’s work revealed that the Great Mound was aligned to the winter solstice. In 1963 O’Kelly discovered a feature at the entrance to the passage that is unique to Newgrange. He called it the roof-box. Its purpose was to channel the rising sun down the passage on the winter solstice and thus to light up the chamber at the heart of the monument.

21 December 1967

On the morning of the winter solstice in 1967, sunlight entered the roof-box and travelled down the passage, illuminating the inner chamber. This confirmed the precise astronomical alignment of Newgrange and demonstrated the advanced knowledge of its Neolithic builders.

1993

Brú na Bóinne was inscribed by UNESCO as a World Heritage Site.

1997

Brú na Bóinne visitor centre opened.

WINTER SOLSTICE

Reveal the winter solstice

The Roof-Box

Newgrange is best known for the illumination of its passage and chamber by the winter solstice sun. Above the entrance is an opening known as the roof-box, designed to allow sunlight to enter the monument on the shortest days of the year.

A Precise Alignment

From December 19th to 23rd, at sunrise, a narrow beam of light passes through the roof-box and travels along the 19 metre passage, gradually filling the inner chamber with light. This event lasts for about 17 minutes and shows the remarkable precision of the monument’s design.

IRISH MEGALITHIC SITES

Beyond Newgrange itself, the Boyne Valley contains a wider ceremonial landscape known as Brú na Bóinne. Alongside Knowth and Dowth, Newgrange forms one of the most important groups of Neolithic monuments in Europe.

Together, these sites show that this was not a single isolated monument, but a connected sacred landscape shaped by organised farming communities over 5,000 years ago.

KNOWTH

The Great Mound

The 5000 year old main mound at Knowth, known as Knowth Site 1, is contemporary with the mounds at Newgrange and Dowth. The site consists of a large mound and 18 smaller satellite mounds. The large mound contains two passages, placed along an east-west line, each leading to a burial chamber.

Megalithic Art

The entire mound is encircled by 127 kerbstones, many of which are decorated with megalithic art. Over 200 decorated stones were found during excavations at Knowth, and the site contains a remarkable concentration of Neolithic art, including spirals, lozenges, concentric circles, and crescent shapes.

DOWTH

A Ceremonial Monument

Dowth is one of the three great passage tombs of Brú na Bóinne, together with Newgrange and Knowth. Together they form one of the most important prehistoric ceremonial landscapes in Europe.

Part of Brú na Bóinne

With the major mounds and smaller surrounding monuments, Dowth helps show that Brú na Bóinne was a connected sacred landscape, built and used over thousands of years.

VISITOR CENTRE

ACCESS

Visitor access to Newgrange and Knowth is strictly by guided tour through the Brú na Bóinne Visitor Centre. There is no direct public access to the monuments themselves. Visits begin with a self guided exhibition before continuing by shuttle bus.

DIRECTIONS

Access to the monuments is via the Brú na Bóinne Visitor Centre, Eircode A92 EH5C. When using GPS, enter the Visitor Centre as your destination.

TICKETING

Pre booking is essential. Access is by guided tour only, with limited numbers on each departure. Tickets are released 30 days in advance during the main season and 7 days in advance during the winter season.
