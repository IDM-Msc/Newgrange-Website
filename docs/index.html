HISTORY OF NEWGRANGE

3500-3000 BC

(Middle Neolithic) The great passage tombs of Knowth, Newgrange and Dowth were built at Brú na Bóinne.

2500-800 BC

(Bronze Age) The Great Stone Circle at Newgrange may have been built early in this period. It is unlikely that the Late Neolithic ceremonial structures were entirely abandoned. However, from around 2000 BC to 100 BC there is little evidence for settlement, burial or ritual at the passage tombs.

800 BC-AD 400

(Iron Age) Burials began again at Knowth around 100 BC. Devotional objects have been found both there and at Newgrange, indicating that both had become important ceremonial sites again.

400-800

The final phase of burials at Knowth began about 600, coinciding with the early years of Christianity in Ireland and the emergence of powerful regional rulers.

1699

Local workmen rediscovered the chamber of the Great Mound at Newgrange.

1882

Knowth, Newgrange and Dowth were listed for protection under the Ancient Monuments Protection Act.

1900s

Archaeological investigation of the passage tombs in Brú na Bóinne began, continuing until the present.

1961

By 1961 Newgrange was in a dilapidated condition and, despite the rapidly increasing number of visitors, there was no right of public access. It was becoming clear to many that this, one of the greatest monuments in Western Europe, could not be left in its then condition, but urgently required restoration and conservation, at least in the entrance area.

1963

At Newgrange, O’Kelly’s work revealed that the Great Mound was aligned to the winter solstice. In 1963 O’Kelly discovered a feature at the entrance to the passage that is unique to Newgrange. He called it the roof-box. Its purpose was to channel the rising sun down the passage on the winter solstice and thus to light up the chamber at the heart of the monument.

21 December 1967

On the morning of the winter solstice in 1967, sunlight entered the roof-box and travelled down the passage, illuminating the inner chamber. This confirmed the precise astronomical alignment of Newgrange and demonstrated the advanced knowledge of its Neolithic builders.

1993

Brú na Bóinne was inscribed by UNESCO as a World Heritage Site.

1997

Brú na Bóinne visitor centre opened.
